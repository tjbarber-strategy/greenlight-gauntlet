The Account in One Paragraph

▾
Greenlight is a $1.42M/month L30 account where TubeScience is losing. TS contributes 35 ads but only $292K (21% of spend) at an average $8,340 per ad — less than half the per-ad spend of both Narrative ($16,771) and Client ($17,640). The portfolio's proven creative DNA — child-POV voice ($530K), scenario-based micro-PAS ($339K), and UGC testimonial video ($520K) — is entirely owned by the client and Narrative. TS has zero representation in the top three mechanisms at scale. The one lane TS owns is Messaging-Hero Statics ($159K, 77.6% creator share), anchored by the NEWS FLASH concept ($198K combined across two ad sets in the launch window). Wallet share has crashed from 30% to 14% since late February, spend collapsed from $118K to $54K, and performance flipped from beating internal to trailing by 14%. This is a creative crisis on an account where TS's survival depends on proving differentiated value within the next 1-2 sprint cycles. The path forward is not competing head-to-head with the client's UGC operation — it's owning the format lanes the client can't or won't produce: carousels, data-driven statics, comedy/skit video, and the dad persona.

What's Working and Why High

▾

1. Child-POV / Kid-as-Protagonist Mechanism — $530K L30, $722K launch

The single most validated mechanism on the account. When the child is the protagonist — speaking, demonstrating, expressing pride — ads scale. This works because it creates an instant pattern interrupt for parents scrolling feed, activates aspirational parenting emotions, and serves as its own social proof. Durability is high: the mechanism carries $530K in the L30 and appears in 22 lifetime ads under the broader "Financial Literacy Transformation Narrative" ($11.4M). TS is entirely absent from this mechanism.

2. Behavioral Proof / Expectation Violation — $290K L30, $198K NEWS FLASH

"NEWS FLASH: Warning — 8-year-olds are asking for more chores." This mechanism works because it violates a universal parental assumption (kids hate chores), generating curiosity while delivering the product's strongest proof point. The NEWS FLASH static is TS's only proven mechanism at scale — $75K in L30, $198K across two conclusive ad sets, ROAS of 2.26-2.40. Format-moated: can't be replicated by handing a script to an influencer.

3. UGC Testimonial from Real Parents — $520K L30, $12.6M lifetime

The account's workhorse format. Narrated B-roll testimonials outperform direct-to-camera by roughly 5:1 in lifetime spend. Entirely owned by Client and Narrative — TS has only $28K in this format.

4. Statics as a Performance Lane — $459K L30, $13.9M lifetime

This account performs unusually well on statics. Card-Design Choice Prompt ($6.3M lifetime, including the #1 all-time ad at $1.65M), NEWS FLASH ($198K launch), and GRL82 notepad static ($307K launch winner). TS's bet on statics is directionally correct, but needs to expand into carousels and data-driven formats.

5. "Mom Hack" / Parenting Shortcut Promise — $107K launch

TS-produced content that won a clean 4-way hook test at 94.4% concentration. The only TS-produced video mechanism that scaled during the launch window. Produced TS Hybrid format — a lane TS can own.

What's Broken: The Key Strategic Problems Critical

▾
🚨 Single-sentence turnaround thesis: TS is losing because it's competing in the client's strength lane (UGC testimonials) instead of owning the lanes the client can't produce (carousels, data-driven statics, comedy/skit video, dad persona creative).

1. TS has no video format at scale — video carries 67% of spend (CG-1)

TubeScience has $84K total across all video formats vs. $857K from Client and Narrative. In UGC Testimonial Video — the #1 format — TS has $28K vs. $492K from others. The J-53 "Mom Hack" infomercial ($107K, 94.4% winner) proves TS CAN make video that works — but it's a single example against a portfolio that needs consistent video output.

2. CBO v2 testing structure starving new creative of spend (MG-1)

Nine concepts launched, $232 total spend — $25 per concept. At ~$150 per registration, zero conversions are possible. This is the #1 revenue blocker. 58 of 93 ad sets land in smoke tier ($4,200 average spend). No creative strategy can overcome a media structure that prevents ads from getting enough delivery to generate signal.

3. TS creative is not differentiated from client output (WG-1)

The client produces UGC testimonials, product demos, and infomercials at higher quality and scale. Narrative produces UGC testimonials and script-length statics. The only format lanes the client and Narrative do NOT produce are carousels and data-driven statics. Carousels are consistently called out as TS's sole differentiated format.

4. Glc-33 concentration risk — 23.9% single-concept dependency (CG-9)

Two Glc-33 child-POV variants carry $339K. Combined with 74.7% spend in a single voice type (UGC Demo) and 66.3% in a single visual style (Elevated Native), the portfolio lacks diversity to absorb fatigue.

5. Bottom funnel essentially nonexistent — retargeting structurally blocked (CG-2, MG-2)

Only 4 ads and $28,582 (2.0%) at BoF. The 180-day visitor exclusion prevents any Meta retargeting. Zero Deal-motivated creative. The "why pay when Chase is free?" objection is completely unaddressed.

TS Value Assessment

▾
Honest assessment: If TS left tomorrow, the client would lose ~21% of portfolio spend ($292K) — but the $75K NEWS FLASH static and most TS output could be replicated. Carousels and the dad persona are the only genuinely defensible moats.

What TS produces that the client can't/won't: (1) Carousels — explicitly confirmed as TS's only differentiated format. (2) Data-driven concept angles — the NEWS FLASH mechanism is strategy-led, not talent-led. (3) Testing velocity on static formats.

TS must prove in the next sprint cycle:

① Carousels can scale as a differentiated format lane — more variants of J66/J79 chore outcomes carousels.

② TS can produce video content that competes — the J-53 "Mom Hack" mechanism needs replication.

③ The dad persona unlock (J75) is real and scalable — this is the single biggest strategic win and TS owns it.

These three things — carousel moat, video viability, and dad persona ownership — are the QBR story.

1. Product Overview

▾

Core offering: Subscription-based family finance platform centered on a kids/teen Mastercard debit card with parental controls, chore-based allowance automation, savings goal tracking, investing (stocks/ETFs with parent approval), financial literacy games, and family safety features (GPS, SOS, crash detection). Plans: Core ($5.99/mo), Max ($10.98/mo), Infinity ($15.98/mo), Family Shield ($19.98-$24.98/mo). Covers up to 5 kids and 2 parents.

Differentiation: Moderate. Most feature-rich product in kids' banking, but core function (kids' debit card) is replicated by free competitors. Differentiation lives in ecosystem depth — investing + chores + savings + safety + education in one app.

Consideration level: Medium. Parents research before committing to a recurring subscription involving their child's finances. Reddit threads, comparison articles, Trustpilot checks are common.

Primary motivator hypothesis: Problem Motivator is the door-opener ("I can't teach my kids about money / I never have cash"). Outcome Motivator is the conversion fuel ("My kid is becoming financially responsible"). Deal Motivator needed defensively given free competitors but should never lead.

2. Executive Summary

▾

Biggest creative opportunity: The "cash is dead but allowance isn't" tension. Every parent has experienced the moment when their kid asks for money and they don't have cash. This is universal, acute, relatable — and it opens the door to everything else Greenlight does.

Most dangerous blind spot: Ignoring the free-competitor pricing objection. Chase, Step, Capital One MONEY all offer core features for $0. Parents doing 30 seconds of research will find free alternatives. Creative must pre-emptively justify why Greenlight's ecosystem is worth $5.99-$15.98/mo.

2-3 consumer truths that should anchor creative:

1. Parents don't buy Greenlight for a debit card — they buy it because their kid acts differently around money.

2. The chore system is the stealth killer feature. Kids asking to do chores and negotiating pay rates.

3. The app functions as a parenting tool that works when parents aren't in the room — real-time notifications, spending controls, and forced trade-offs teach lessons passively.

5. Audience Map

▾

Audience 1: "My kid keeps asking me to buy stuff and I always say yes or no with no lesson." The parent whose cashless lifestyle makes money invisible. Broad TAM.

Audience 2: "I never have cash for allowance and the envelope system fell apart." The organized parent whose manual system failed. Broad TAM.

Audience 3: "My teen is going on a trip and I need to give them money I can monitor." Acute, time-bound need. Medium TAM, high urgency.

Audience 4: "I want my kid to start learning about investing early." Financially engaged parent. Niche TAM.

Audience 5: "Grandparent who wants to contribute from a distance." Underserved. Medium-niche TAM.

UNTAPPED: "Parent whose kid just got their first phone (ages 10-12)." Phone = apps = wanting to buy things online. No competitor targets this moment.

6-7. Motivator Taxonomy & Anti-Competitors

▾

Problem Motivators

"I never have cash for allowance" — universal, chronic. Strong standalone hook.

"My kid thinks money is infinite" — tap-to-pay culture. Broad TAM, strong fear angle.

"Nobody taught me about money" — generational guilt. Deep, identity-level. Needs Outcome proof to resolve.

Outcome Motivators

"My kid actually wants to do chores now" — sounds too good to be true, which makes it compelling. Multiple testimonials.

"My kid is becoming financially responsible on their own" — identity-level for parents. Emotional peak of the Greenlight story.

Anti-Competitor Triggers

"Cash allowance is impossible to track" — switching from physical cash. Universal.

"The bank account had no parental controls" — switching from youth checking accounts.

"The free option was too basic" — switching from Chase/Capital One. Ecosystem depth wins.

8. Barriers to Purchase

▾

Barrier 1 — "What will I get?" Confusion about day-to-day experience. Response: 60-second product demo.

Barrier 2 — "How does it work?" Setup friction. Response: "Card arrives in days, app takes 5 minutes."

Barrier 3 — "Is this the smart decision?" Free alternatives found within 2 minutes. Dealbreaker for price-sensitive. Response: ecosystem reframe + $1.20/kid/month.

Barrier 6 — "Can I afford it?" $5.99/mo vs. free. Dealbreaker. Response: price anchoring — less than one kids' meal, $72/year vs. $1,819/year cost of illiteracy.

Barrier 7 — "How risky is it?" Cancellation horror stories (20 reviews). Dealbreaker for researchers. Response: "Cancel anytime, no fees, first month free."

Barrier 8 — "Why should I trust this?" Fraud complaints, BBB "B" rating, CFPB investigation. Response: "6.5 million parents trust Greenlight. FDIC-insured. 4.8★ from 479K ratings."

9-10. Competitive Landscape & Strategy

▾

Direct Competitors

Chase First Banking (FREE): Powered by Greenlight technology. Lacks investing, literacy games, multi-parent, store-level controls. "Chase runs on Greenlight's tech" is the strongest credibility asset.

Step (FREE): Ages 13+. Only competitor that builds actual credit history. No parental controls, no chores, no investing.

Acorns Early ($5-10/mo): Per-child pricing weakness. Greenlight wins for 3+ kid families.

Capital One MONEY (FREE): No chores, no investing, no education. "It's free and has more ATMs."

Market Context

Only 15-25% of 33M U.S. families use a dedicated kids' fintech — 75% headroom remains. 29 states now mandate financial literacy education. Greenlight's B2B2C strategy (200+ bank partners) is the most significant structural moat.
